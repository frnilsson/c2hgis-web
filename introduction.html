Introduction

Consumers rely on many different types of resources to manage their health and everyday lives. Most of our lives - in illness and in health - are spent in our homes and communities, and outside of traditional healthcare systems. Broadband has the potential to connect individuals and communities to the people, information, and resources they need to get well and stay healthy. Broadband ecosystems and networks can work to enable health and other benefits. They can enable clinicians, social services providers, pharmacies, grocery stores, fitness centers, and other siloed resources to work collaboratively and seamlessly with each other around the needs of individuals to reduce their risk of disease, reach personal health goals, and optimize health outcomes.

Health is a complex construct that is determined by an interplay of factors that may be different for each individual. The health of a population can be approximated by looking at specific health related measures such as prevalence of obesity or the number of physicians in the region. Broadband as well is a nuanced construct, including upload and download speeds and functional characteristics like number of providers in an area, actual versus advertised speeds, among others.

An interactive tool that looks at both connectivity and health can show a picture of gaps and opportunities in the broadband ecosystem. We can use this tool to generate insights and answer questions such as:

  • What is the picture of health (i.e. need) in areas with low connectivity (i.e. resources)?
  • What is the picture of health/needs in areas with high connectivity/resources?
  • How does the picture of need change if we go from areas with low to high connectivity/resources?
  • Where are the gaps that broadband networks can fill?
  • Where are the needs that various parts of the broadband ecosystem can meet?

By asking these questions we get an understanding of how one or more of the elements of a broadband ecosystem can potentially enable health for populations. For example, rural areas with low connectivity and high diabetes may benefit from improved networks while another area with good connectivity and high diabetes may benefit from an intervention around devices and applications. These insights can be useful to a broad range of stakeholders that can benefit from making networks work and enabling health through broadband.
